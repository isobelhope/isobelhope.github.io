Komondors


Komondors (also known as the Hungarian sheepdog, or mop dogs) were bred (and are used) as livestock guardians. The oldest reference to this breed is in the Hungarian codex in 1544, and in the 20th century were declared a Hungarian National Treasure, making them protected from modification. They are related to the Old English Sheepdog.
They are large dogs, with their most distinctive feature being their heavy matted cords, which can reach around 25 cm in length each. The fur has a further internal layer, which is much softer than the cords. In total, these two layers sum to the heaviest fur of the canine world. Traditionally, this fur was thick enough to protect to dogs from wolf bites, which made them invaluable protection for the livestock. Once wet, the coat takes three days to dry properly.
The dogs themselves weigh around 50 kg, with a broad head. They tend to be fairly calm and stable, but in times of stress will act independently of their owners. They are fiercely protective of their home and family, and can hold down intruders until the owners arrive.
In 2020, there were 0 registrations with the UK Kennel Club.



Otterhounds


Otterhounds are a British scent hound – they can track a scent through mud and water for over three days. As the name suggests, this breed was for hunting otters, which makes them able to carry their quarry through water or on land. They have a very oily double coat and extremely webbed feet, with a large head and very sensitive nose. This sensitive nose makes them inquisitive, and so are in general fairly tractable, though once they get an interesting scent they are hard to discourage.
They were first referenced in writings in 1360, although a distinct breed can only be traced back to the early 1800s. Otter hunting was never outright banned in the UK, but the otter hunts voluntarily stopped when they noticed the severe decline in otter population. They then notified the authorities, who placed the otter on the list of protected species in 1978. As the Otterhounds had been so selectively bred for their hunting capabilities, few of them were suitable as companions, and so their population declined very quickly. Because of this, the Kennel Club have registered the breed as a vulnerable native breed, with only 600 animals worldwide.
They live for around 10 years. They can handle considerable exercise but will often be very happy in an unhealthy state of laziness. This breed is relatively prone to epilepsy and seizures, which are usually fatal.
There were 7 registrations of Otterhounds in the UK with the Kennel Club in 2020.

Australian Silky Terrier


This breed of terrier was developed in Australia from UK exports of Yorkshire terriers. The breed was established by the end of the 19th century. They are small, compact and short legged, with a distinctive coat of long grey or tan silk, typically styled hanging straight down and parted down the back. The coat is usually described as “flat, fine, and glossy”. These dogs were bred originally for hunting rodents and snakes, before quickly becoming more of a pet. The breed standard for these dogs is very alert and active, requiring a lot of stimulation and exercise. They are intelligent dogs, but tend to be stubborn and wilful, which makes training quite difficult. They are independent, but loyal to their owners, and so bark often at incoming strangers.
Because of their coat, they require a lot of grooming. They are also particularly prone to dry and itchy skin, and the use of a harness (as opposed to a choke lead) is encouraged, to prevent tracheal collapse.
There were 4 registrations of Australian Silky Terrier with the UK Kennel Club in 2020.
